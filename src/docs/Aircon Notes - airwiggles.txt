How loud should my audio be?
* Bill Lacey


How loud are you listening?
Consider:
   * Most people are not sure
   * Most rooms sound different
   * Hearing safety
Uncertainty can lead to:
* Inconsistent results
* Ineffective results
* Listening fatigue
* Hearing damage
Fletcher-Munson - Equal Loudness Contours
* Our ears do not perceive frequencies the same way at all listening levels
   * When listening softer, the bass and treble frequencies will appear to be softer than they really are
   * When listening louder, the reverse is true
   * At roughly 80-85 db SPL, our ears perceive high and low frequencies accurately in proportion to mid-range frequencies. This should be your target.
  

  

Listen at a reference level
* In proportion to the size of your listening space
* 85 db is for theatrical stages approximately 20000 cubic feet
* Smaller the room, the closer you are to the speakers, the lower your reference level should be
* Bill’s room is 15.5 W * 17.5 L * 8.5 H = 2300 cubic feet
   * 78 dB
How do I set my levels?
You need a sound-level meter - amazon, radioshack, etc. check ebay
* Set it to slow, C weighting
Free app from national institute for occupational safety and health (NIOSH): loudness over time
Understanding loudness part 3 - calibrating your monitors - Mike Thornton
https://youlean.co/online-loudness-meter/
1. Pink noise generator
   1. Send through one speaker
   2. Set up sound-level meter, pointed toward speakers
2. Headphones?
   1. Work on monitors/speakers, and reference with headphones
   2. No official practices on loudness measurements with headphones
   3. DIY, not as scientific
      1. 4 ways to measure headphone loudness levels (Decibel) - video
3. Use dialogue as a reference
   1. Dialogue should usually sit at about -24 dB LUFS in your daw. You can try switching back and forth from your monitors and headphones to see what level sounds right in your headphones
4. Once you have established the correct listening levels for your environment, don’t change the level
   1. Unless you have an extremely good reason to do so
Loudness Meters
* Used to determine how loud our ears perceive sound over time
   * VisLM - NUGEN Audio
   * YOULEAN LOUDNESS METER-2
Two useful measurements
* short-term/momentary value
   * In the past few seconds
* long-term/integrated value
   * Average over the course of what you’re listening to
Specs you are provided will refer to the long-term/integrated measurement
The longer the program measurement, the easier it is to accommodate delivery specs
Being in spec doesn’t mean your mix is great
Challenges
* Different loudness specs by region, network, platform
   * Sometimes delivery requires multiple specs
* Recommendations
   * Work in your daw to -24 dB LUFS
   * Put loudness meter as the last plugin in your master fader
      * Average dialogue should fit in -24 dB
   * Never change the balance between your speaker loudness and your DAW
   * In that context, sound that is meant to be soft will translate properly
   * Sounds that are supposed to be loud will sound loud, and meters will support that
   * For continuously loud content (a mix or trailer destined for youtube at -14, etc.), create separate output paths and use a limiter to increase the level to match the delivery spec.
      * Don’t listen at -14.
      * Bounce it, turn volume down, and listen

History of Sonic Notification:
* Church bells
   * In addition to call to worship, church bells were used to inform residents about
      * Weddings and funerals
      * Victory celebrations
      * Clock chimes
      * Warnings
* Doorbells
   * 1800s: replaced door knockers, and became widespread soonafter
   * 1850s: integration into telephone systems
   * 1930s: wireless doorbells used radio
   * 1950s: multi-tone chimes
   * 1970s: introduction of videos
   * 2010s: smart doorbell system
* Elevator sonification
   * Elevator operator’s strike in 1870s: forced move toward automation of the system
      * 1892: Buttons and chimes were introduced to communicate information
      * 1934: Musak (elevator music) - created to calm the user and help them relax
      * 1950: first operator-less elevator
      * 1962: fully automatic elevator
* Alarms and sirens
   * Hearing the correct notification could be life-or-death
      * Function and urgency is important
      * Alarms are designed with dissonance and atypical rhythms to intentionally startle
      * Sirens are designed with frequency modulation so the listener doesn’t get used to a single tone

So you want to release a sound library…
Who
Customer or audience
* Think like a sound editor
   * Game vs linear needs
   * String-outs vs single files
      * Going in the direction of string-outs
   * What are the holes in others’ libraries? - fill those holes
Why
Reverse order of appropriate motivations:
* Money
* Reputation
* Relationship building
* Practice
* Passion
Importance of a brief:
* Write the product description before creating the library
   * Change it as ideas and library evolves
What
Research: 2 approaches…
* Screw research, just be inspired
   * Follow passion and don’t bother with research
   * What do you struggle to find in your own library?
   * What resources do you have access to?
* Get some data
   * Know the market
   * Gap analysis: holes in the market, in perspective, content, etc.
   * Ask your colleagues or community what they want
Do both.
Concept
* Evergreen vs new territory
* Designed vs “construction kit” of parts to be put together
* Make it unique: subject, texture, perspective, technique, etc.
* Make or use creative limitations
How
Organizing
* Never throw raw files away
* Ensure you can roll back changes or versions
* Retain all edited files even if you don’t ship them in the library
Recording
* Do the best with what you have - every room has issues to address
* use practice sessions and “throwaway work”
* Scale your variations to your market
* A word about noise: noise should be consistent. Can be there, because when it is put in context, it can often be forgiven
* Backup your stuff
Designing
* You do you
* Don’t do anything your customers will wish you hadn’t
   * Don’t go too aggressive with processing, etc.
   * Be clear about what you are offering. Don’t make your customer wish you could undo something
* Don’t overwhelm the customer with mic choice: making your solution not a solution. Too complicated
* Backup your stuff
Mastering
* Bit depth: 24 cap
* Headroom varies, but just don’t clip
* Loudness consistency: make sound events consistent proportionally to other sound events, as expected in life
* The material tells you what it wants
* Do no harm: the people who use it will throw crazy effects on it in their work, and you don’t want your stuff to easily clip
* Backup your stuff
Metadata
* Customer expectations: formatting, metadata fields, finding a sound at the right time
* UCS v8.2 and Kai Paquin’s metadata guide
* Categorize by most important attribute (are the birds or the forest more important for use cases?)
* Consider how editors might search for it
Sound miner
Etc.
* You don’t have to do this alone
* You’re making “soundware”, treat it like software
   * Alpha testing
   * Beta testing
* Make everything you do shippable
When
What is “Done”?
* Resolution > completion
* Kill your darlings
* QC pitfalls
   * Spelling and factual errors in metadata or marketing
   * Filler assets
   * Verbal slates left in recordings
   * Uneven loudness for similar sound events
   * No actual keywords (individual words)
Last decisions
* Did you meet your goals?
* Do you want to ask others for feedback?
* Will you have the time and energy to act upon feedback?
* You never have to release anything
Marketing
* No one buys things they don’t know about
* Market is small
* Market is incredibly crowded
* Promoting yourself is always awkward
Phases of marketing
* Pre-release: announce
* Release: celebrate
* Post-release: remind
* “The drum beat” of ongoing marketing
Marketing collateral
Must haves:
* Product image
* Sound demo
* Metadata list (PDF)
* EULA (PDF)
   * Read the EULAs you have, and talk to an IP lawyer
Nice-to-haves:
* Teasers
* Release video/trailer
* BTS featurette videos
* “in-use” videos/demos
* booklets/PDFs
Where
Commerce platform
* Distribution vs direct sales
   * Website
   * Customer service
   * Mailing list
   * Marketing support
* Your time vs your money
Pricing
* Availability
* Comparable product prices
* Value offered
Conclusion
* Libraries are always worth doing, even if you don’t release them
* Rewards here are often not monetary
